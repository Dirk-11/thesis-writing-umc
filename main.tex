\begin{abstract}
Kidney stone composition determines treatment strategy and recurrence risk, yet all existing automated approaches assign stones to discrete mineral categories, discarding the continuous compositional information measured by Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy. We present the first deep learning study to predict continuous FTIR-calibrated mineral composition percentages directly from ex vivo kidney stone photographs.
 
We trained three models on 414 stones from the MOCKI dataset (Amsterdam UMC): a binary pure/mixed classifier (Model A), a compositional regression model predicting continuous five-class mineral fractions using KL divergence loss with an auxiliary presence detection head (Model B), and a multiclass dominant-class classifier serving as a controlled baseline (Model C). All models share a ResNet18 backbone with two-phase staged fine-tuning and are evaluated under five-fold cross-validation and a held-out test set.
 
Model A distinguished pure from mixed stones with AUC = 0.715 on the test set at the 100\% purity threshold, with performance biased toward the majority class at both thresholds evaluated. Model B predicted calcium oxalate and calcium phosphate fractions with Spearman correlations of 0.72 and 0.56, respectively, and achieved dominant-class macro F1 of 0.483---comparable to Model C (0.430)---while additionally providing continuous composition estimates. Model B outperformed Model C on calcium phosphate identification (F1 $\approx$ 0.58 vs. $\approx$ 0.38), suggesting that learning the full composition vector improves representation of co-occurring mineral classes. Model C retained an advantage on rare classes due to its class-weighted loss.
 
These results establish that continuous mineral composition is learnable from stone photographs and that the regression framing is not only theoretically motivated but practically competitive with direct classification. Current accuracy is not yet sufficient for clinical deployment, but the approach provides a foundation for future work with larger datasets and extension to in vivo endoscopic imaging.
\end{abstract}
\end{@twocolumnfalse}

\section*{Introduction}
% ============================================================

\subsection*{Kidney stone disease and the importance of composition}

Kidney stone disease is one of the most common urological conditions worldwide, with incidence rates ranging from 1\% in parts of Asia to 13\% in North America and a global trend of rising prevalence over the past three decades \cite{Türk2022, Qian2022, Sorokin2017, Stamatelou2023}. In 2021 alone, an estimated 106 million new cases were recorded globally \cite{Qian2022}. Recurrence rates are high---up to 50\% of patients experience a second episode within ten years---and the associated morbidity, from acute renal colic to long-term kidney damage, places a substantial burden on healthcare systems.

Preventing recurrence requires knowing what the stone is made of. Different mineral compositions reflect different underlying metabolic disorders, and treatment decisions---dietary modification, urine alkalinisation, or pharmacotherapy---depend directly on this information \cite{Daudon2016, Corrales2021}. The reference method for compositional analysis is Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR), which can accurately identify the chemical nature and relative proportions of each mineral component \cite{Basiri2025, Daudon2016}. However, FTIR is performed ex vivo after the stone has been retrieved and requires specialised laboratory equipment and trained personnel. Results typically take days to weeks to reach the clinician, limiting its utility for timely treatment decisions.

\subsection*{Towards automated visual recognition}

An alternative is to estimate composition from the stone's visual appearance. Clinicians with extensive training can infer composition from surface morphology, texture, and colour---an approach known as endoscopic stone recognition (ESR), formalised in the Daudon morpho-constitutional classification system \cite{Daudon2016, Corrales2021}. In practice, however, ESR requires years of experience and shows poor agreement between observers \cite{Randall2023}, which makes it unsuitable for routine use.

This is where deep learning becomes relevant. Over the past five years, several groups have shown that convolutional neural networks (CNNs) can classify kidney stone types from photographs with reasonable accuracy. Most of this work has focused on in vivo endoscopic images: the group of Ochoa-Ruiz and Daul explored CNN-based classification, two-step transfer learning, and metric learning on ureteroscopic images \cite{LopezTiro2021, LopezTiro2024, GonzalezZapata2024}, while Estrade and colleagues demonstrated recognition of pure and mixed stone morphologies from both still images and video \cite{Estrade2022, Estrade2022video}. Other groups have worked with ex vivo photographs taken after stone extraction: Black et al. were among the first to apply deep learning to this setting \cite{Black2020}, Kim et al. predicted the presence of individual mineral components \cite{Kim2022}, and Serrat et al. built an automated classification pipeline for expelled stones \cite{Serrat2017}. More recently, Zhu et al. trained models on intraoperative images for real-time composition prediction \cite{Zhu2024}, Leng et al. combined segmentation and classification from endoscopic video \cite{Leng2024}, and Onal and Tekgul explored smartphone-based microscopy \cite{Onal2022}. A comprehensive benchmark of these approaches was provided by El Beze et al. \cite{ElBeze2022}.

\subsection*{The gap: classification vs. continuous composition}

Despite this progress, all existing approaches frame the problem as a \textit{classification} task. The model assigns each stone to a fixed category---a single mineral type or a predefined mixture type from the Daudon system---and outputs a discrete label. This formulation discards an important part of the clinical picture: the continuous mineralogical composition measured by FTIR.

Most kidney stones are in fact mixtures of two or more minerals, and the relative proportions matter clinically \cite{Daudon2016}. A stone that is 90\% calcium oxalate monohydrate (COM) with 10\% calcium phosphate (CaP) suggests a different metabolic profile than one that is 60\% COM and 40\% CaP, yet both would receive the same label under existing classification systems. To our knowledge, no prior study has attempted to predict continuous FTIR-calibrated mineral composition percentages directly from stone photographs. This gap motivates the present work.

\subsection*{Research question}

The central question of this thesis is: \textit{can a deep learning model predict the continuous mineral composition of a kidney stone from its ex vivo photograph, as calibrated by FTIR spectroscopy?}

We address this through two sub-questions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item How accurately can a binary classifier distinguish pure from mixed stones based on visual features alone?
    \item How well does a compositional regression model predict the dominant mineral class compared to a conventional multiclass classifier trained on the same data?
\end{enumerate}

We work with ex vivo photographs---taken immediately after stone retrieval, outside the body---as a deliberate first step before tackling the harder in vivo endoscopic setting. Prior work from this group established this imaging protocol and demonstrated that mineral class discrimination is feasible on this dataset \cite{veltheer2025}. The present study builds on that foundation by replacing discrete classification with continuous compositional prediction.

\section*{Methods}
% ============================================================

\subsection*{Study design}

We trained and compared three deep learning models, each addressing a different aspect of the composition profiling problem (Figure~\ref{fig:workflow}):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Model A}: a binary classifier that distinguishes pure from mixed stones,
    \item \textbf{Model B}: a compositional regression model that predicts continuous mineral percentages,
    \item \textbf{Model C}: a multiclass classifier that predicts the dominant mineral class.
\end{itemize}

Model A addresses the most basic clinical question: does this stone contain a mixture of minerals? This is relevant because pure and mixed stones may require different treatment strategies and follow-up. Model B is the central contribution of this thesis. It is an attempt to predict continuous FTIR-calibrated composition from photographs rather than a categorical label. Model C exists as a controlled baseline for Model B: both models can be evaluated on dominant class prediction, isolating the effect of the regression framing from the architecture and training procedure.


All three models share the same backbone architecture and training procedure; they differ only in their output head and loss function. This design allows a controlled comparison: Model B and Model C can both be evaluated on dominant class prediction, isolating the effect of the regression framing. The full pipeline is config-driven and publicly available at \url{https://github.com/Dirkdeboer/umc_thesis}.

\subsection*{Dataset}

The dataset consists of 423 kidney stone fragments collected at Amsterdam UMC (VUmc) as part of the MOCKI study. Each fragment was photographed ex vivo immediately after retrieval, yielding three photographs per stone (1269 images total). Stones were placed on a black background and photographed under controlled lighting with a digital camera. Two stones were excluded due to missing FTIR labels, and a further 7 lacked composition data entirely, leaving 414 stones with valid FTIR-calibrated composition vectors.

Mineral composition was determined by FTIR spectroscopy following standard morpho-constitutional analysis \cite{Daudon2016}, producing continuous percentage values for each component present in the stone. The raw FTIR output distinguishes nine mineral classes.

\subsection*{Data preprocessing}

The raw clinical data required several cleaning steps before it could be used for modelling. Fragment identifiers were forward-filled across photo rows, as only the first photo of each stone carried the identifier in the original spreadsheet. Known data-entry errors in the percentage columns were corrected based on clinical verification (six stones). Photo identifiers with formatting inconsistencies---missing prefixes, single-letter abbreviations---were standardised, and trailing whitespace in component labels was stripped.

\subsubsection*{Class consolidation}

We consolidated the nine raw FTIR classes into six target classes through a two-stage remapping. In the first stage, we examined stones labelled as ``OTH'' (other) using the FTIR sub-identification field. Where the mineral could be chemically identified, we reassigned it to the appropriate class: urate salts and uric acid dihydrate (30 stones) were assigned to UA, calcium-magnesium phosphate (4 stones) to CaP, and newberyite (1 stone) to MAP. The remaining OTH stones---calcite, protein, and xanthine---stayed as OTH because they have no clear chemical relationship to the major classes. In the second stage, we merged subtypes into final classes: COM and COD into CaOx, CHP, CHPD, and CMP into CaP, with UA, MAP, and CYS remaining as individual classes.

For Models B and C, the OTH class was excluded because it is chemically heterogeneous and too small (4 stones with OTH as dominant component) to train or evaluate reliably, leaving 5 target classes across 410 stones.

\subsubsection*{Composition vectors and purity labels}

For each stone, we built a composition vector by summing the FTIR percentages from all positions (primary, secondary, tertiary) into the corresponding class bucket, then normalising the vector to sum to 1.0. This normalised vector serves as the regression target for Model B, and its argmax provides the dominant class label for Model C.

Stones whose composition was 100\% a single mineral were labelled \textit{pure}; all others were labelled \textit{mixed}. This strict threshold was chosen in consultation with the urologists at Amsterdam UMC, who consider any detectable mixture clinically relevant. Prior literature has used a more lenient threshold of 90\% \cite{Kim2022}; we evaluate Model A at both thresholds and compare the effect on classification performance.

\subsubsection*{Data splitting}
 
All splits were performed at the stone level to prevent data leakage between the three photos of the same stone. For each model, we first held out 20\% of stones as a test set using stratified sampling, then divided the remaining 80\% into five folds using stratified $k$-fold cross-validation. The stratification variable was chosen to match each model's target: for Model A, both the test set and folds were stratified on the binary pure/mixed label; for Models B and C, stratification was on the dominant mineral class. Because Models B and C share the same stratification variable, sample pool (excluding OTH-dominant stones), and random seed, their fold assignments and test sets are identical, enabling a direct per-fold comparison. Model A operates on a slightly different sample pool (including OTH-dominant stones) with different stratification, so its test set is not identical to that of Models B and C. The test set was used only for final evaluation.

\subsection*{Image preprocessing and augmentation}

Images were resized to $224 \times 224$ pixels. Before normalisation, we applied a background masking step: pixels whose mean RGB intensity fell below a threshold of 20/255 were zeroed out. This prevents the model from learning features of the black imaging plate rather than the stone surface. After masking, pixel values were normalised using ImageNet channel statistics.

During training, we applied data augmentation to increase the effective dataset size: random horizontal and vertical flips (each with probability 0.5), random rotation up to 45 degrees, and colour jitter (brightness and contrast $\pm$0.4, saturation $\pm$0.2). Because stones are photographed ex vivo on a flat surface, their orientation is arbitrary, which justifies the aggressive geometric augmentation. Colour jitter accounts for variation in lighting across imaging sessions. No augmentation was applied during validation or testing.

\subsection*{Model architecture}

All three models use ResNet18 \cite{He2016} pretrained on ImageNet as their backbone. ResNet18 is an 18-layer residual network with skip connections that enable stable training of deep networks. We chose ResNet18 over the larger ResNet50---used in most prior kidney stone studies \cite{LopezTiro2024, veltheer2025}---because with only $\sim$420 stones, the larger model is prone to overfitting. The backbone's original classification head is removed, leaving a 512-dimensional feature vector from the global average pooling layer. Each model attaches its own task-specific output head to this feature vector.

\subsubsection*{Model A: binary classifier}

Model A adds a dropout layer followed by a single linear layer that maps the feature vector to two outputs (pure vs. mixed), trained with weighted cross-entropy loss. To address the class imbalance at the 100\% purity threshold---where mixed stones form 65\% of the dataset---we applied inverse-frequency class weighting to the cross-entropy loss. The weight for each class was set proportional to the inverse of its frequency in the training fold, upweighting pure stones to compensate for their underrepresentation. At inference time, the predicted probability of the mixed class is averaged across the three photos of a stone, and a threshold of 0.5 is applied.

\subsubsection*{Model B: compositional regression}

Model B is the central model of this thesis. It predicts a continuous five-dimensional composition vector, where each element represents the estimated fraction of a mineral class (CaOx, CaP, UA, MAP, CYS). The architecture has two output heads that share the same backbone.

The \textit{composition head} maps the feature vector through a hidden layer (512 $\to$ 128 units with ReLU and dropout) to five outputs, which are then passed through a softmax function:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{c}_k = \frac{\exp(z_k)}{\sum_{j=1}^{5} \exp(z_j)}, \quad k = 1, \ldots, 5
\end{equation}
This enforces the compositional constraint: all predicted fractions are non-negative and sum to 1, making them directly comparable to the FTIR composition vectors. It is important to note that this softmax serves as a simplex constraint, not as a classification mechanism---the task is regression on a compositional target, not classification.

The composition head is trained with Kullback--Leibler (KL) divergence between the predicted and true composition:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{KL}} = \sum_{k=1}^{5} c_k \log \frac{c_k}{\hat{c}_k}
\end{equation}
We chose KL divergence over simpler alternatives like mean absolute error because it is the natural loss for probability distributions on the simplex. In practical terms, KL divergence penalises confidently wrong predictions more heavily than small errors in already-uncertain classes, which is desirable when the model needs to get the dominant component right while still capturing minority fractions.

The \textit{auxiliary presence head} has the same architecture but outputs independent sigmoid activations, one per class, predicting whether each mineral is present in the stone. This head is trained with binary cross-entropy loss and provides an additional training signal that encourages the backbone to learn features sensitive to minority components, that might otherwise be ignored because their fractions are small. The total loss combines both terms with a weight of 0.3 on the auxiliary loss. This value was selected from \{0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0\} based on validation macro F1 and Spearman's $\rho$
(Appendix~\ref{app:lambda}).

At inference time, composition predictions from the three photos of a stone are averaged and re-normalised to sum to 1.

\subsubsection*{Model C: multiclass classifier}

Model C has the same architecture as Model A (dropout + linear) but outputs five logits, one per mineral class. The target label is the dominant component in the FTIR composition vector. Because the dataset is imbalanced---CaOx accounts for roughly 48\% of stones while MAP represents less than 3\%---we used inverse-frequency class weighting in the cross-entropy loss to prevent the model from simply predicting the majority class.

Model C serves as a direct baseline for Model B. Both models can be evaluated on dominant class prediction, which isolates the question of whether predicting continuous compositions offers any advantage over predicting a single class label directly.

At inference time, class probabilities from the three photos are averaged and the class with the highest mean probability is selected.

\subsection*{Training procedure}

All models were trained using a two-phase staged fine-tuning strategy, following the approach used in prior kidney stone classification work \cite{LopezTiro2024, veltheer2025}. In the first phase, we froze the backbone and trained only the output head for 5 epochs. This gives the head a chance to learn a reasonable mapping from ImageNet features before the backbone weights are modified. In the second phase, we unfroze the entire network and trained end-to-end with a much lower learning rate for the backbone than for the head. The idea is that the pre-trained features are already useful and should be adjusted gently rather than overwritten. Training continued for up to 20--25 epochs with early stopping based on validation loss. A summary of all hyperparameters is given in Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams}.

\subsection*{Evaluation}

All metrics are computed at the stone level after aggregating predictions across the three photos.

For Model A, we report AUC-ROC, precision, recall, and F1 score. For Model B, we report the mean absolute error (MAE) between predicted and true composition vectors, both per class and overall. To compare Model B directly against Model C, we also derive a dominant class prediction from Model B by taking the class with the highest predicted fraction, and compute macro-F1 and per-class F1 for both models on this shared task. For the auxiliary presence head, we report per-class precision, recall, and F1 separately. For Model C, we report macro-F1 and per-class F1.

\subsection*{Code and data availability}

All code, configuration files, and training scripts are publicly available at \url{https://github.com/Dirkdeboer/umc_thesis}. The image data and FTIR labels are held at Amsterdam UMC and are not publicly available, but may be accessible on request for academic research.

\section*{Results}
 
\subsection*{Dataset characteristics}
 
After preprocessing and class consolidation, the dataset comprised 414 stones with valid composition vectors across six mineral classes (Figure~\ref{fig:presence}). CaOx and CaP together appeared in over half of all stones, both as primary and secondary components, reflecting their dominance in clinical kidney stone populations. UA, MAP, CYS, and OTH were less common, each appearing as the primary component in fewer than 10\% of stones. Notably, CaP appeared as a secondary or tertiary component in nearly as many stones as CaOx, underscoring that mixed compositions are the norm rather than the exception in this dataset.
 
At the 100\% purity threshold, 146 stones (35\%) were labelled pure and 268 (65\%) mixed. At the 90\% threshold, this shifted to 229 pure (55\%) and 185 mixed (45\%), illustrating how sensitive the class balance is to the threshold definition.

\subsection*{Model A: binary classification of pure vs. mixed stones}

Model A achieved an AUC of 0.746 $\pm$ 0.044 on cross-validation at the 100\% purity threshold, compared to 0.705 $\pm$ 0.093 at the 90\% threshold (Figure~\ref{fig:roc}). Both thresholds substantially outperformed the random-label baseline (AUC = 0.502 $\pm$ 0.061), confirming that the model captures genuine visual features related to stone purity. On the held-out test set, the pattern was consistent: AUC = 0.715 at the 100\% threshold and 0.703 at 90\%.

The 100\% threshold performed slightly better and with lower variance across folds. This may seem counterintuitive---a stricter threshold means fewer pure stones, making the task harder---but it also makes the distinction sharper: a stone is pure only if it contains absolutely no secondary minerals, giving the model a cleaner signal to learn from. At 90\%, the boundary between pure and mixed becomes fuzzier, and stones near the threshold may look visually similar regardless of which side they fall on.

Even with inverse-frequency class weighting, the model shows residual bias toward the majority class at each threshold (Figure~\ref{fig:confusion}). At the 100\% threshold, recall on mixed stones remains higher than on pure stones (90\% vs.\ 44\%); at the 90\% threshold, the pattern reverses (77\% pure recall vs.\ 58\% mixed). This residual asymmetry reflects the visual difficulty of the task near the boundary: stones that are borderline pure or mixed look nearly identical regardless of which side of the threshold they fall on. Class weighting can shift the decision boundary but cannot recover visual information that is not present in the image.

\subsection*{Model B: compositional regression}
 
The composition model learned to predict continuous mineral fractions with varying accuracy across classes (Figure~\ref{fig:violin}). On the held-out test set, CaOx showed the strongest performance (MAE = 0.269, $\rho$ = 0.72), followed by CaP (MAE = 0.247, $\rho$ = 0.56). This is consistent with these two classes making up the majority of the dataset and having the widest range of true fractions for the model to learn from. UA, MAP, and CYS had lower MAE values (0.137, 0.052, and 0.099 respectively), partly because their true fractions are concentrated near zero for most stones, but their rank correlations were moderate ($\rho$ = 0.45--0.48), suggesting the model captures the relative ordering of stones even for minority classes.
 
The violin plot reveals an important asymmetry in the errors. CaOx predictions are skewed below zero, meaning the model tends to underestimate calcium oxalate fractions. CaP shows the opposite pattern---a positive bias, with the model overestimating calcium phosphate. Since CaOx and CaP frequently co-occur in mixed stones and their fractions must sum to 1 (along with the other classes), this suggests the model struggles to correctly partition the composition between these two dominant classes. For the rarer classes (UA, MAP, CYS), errors are more symmetric and concentrated near zero.

\subsection*{Model B vs. Model C: dominant class prediction}
 
To answer the second sub-question---whether the compositional regression model can match a direct multiclass classifier at predicting the dominant mineral---we compared their per-class F1 scores on the held-out test set (Figure~\ref{fig:f1summary}). Both models achieved a comparable macro F1 of approximately 0.45, substantially above the random-label baseline ($\sim$0.13), which confirms that both learned meaningful representations.
 
The per-class breakdown reveals a more nuanced picture. For CaOx, the two models performed nearly identically (F1 $\approx$ 0.70), which is expected given that calcium oxalate is the largest class and visually distinctive. For CaP, however, Model B clearly outperformed Model C (F1 $\approx$ 0.58 vs. $\approx$ 0.38). This is a notable finding: by learning the full composition vector rather than just the dominant label, Model B appears to develop a richer representation of calcium phosphate stones that also improves its ability to identify CaP as the dominant component. Model C showed stronger performance on CYS (F1 $\approx$ 0.57 vs. $\approx$ 0.00 for Model B) and MAP (F1 $\approx$ 0.21 vs. $\approx$ 0.00), both of which are rare classes. This is likely because the class-weighted cross-entropy loss explicitly compensates for class imbalance, whereas Model B's KL divergence loss has no such mechanism---it optimises overall composition accuracy, which is dominated by the common classes.

\section*{Discussion}
% ============================================================
 
We set out to investigate whether continuous mineral composition can be predicted from ex vivo kidney stone photographs, as an alternative to the discrete classification framing used in all prior automated approaches. The answer is yes, with important caveats. Model B learns to rank mineral fractions with Spearman correlations of up to 0.72 for calcium oxalate and 0.56 for calcium phosphate, and achieves dominant-class macro F1 on par with a dedicated multiclass classifier (0.483 vs. 0.430 on the held-out test set). These results establish compositional regression as a viable and informative task, even on a dataset of the size available here.
 
\subsection*{Binary classification depends on the purity threshold}
 
Model A achieved AUC values of 0.715 and 0.703 on the held-out test set at the 100\% and 90\% purity thresholds, respectively, with cross-validation AUCs of 0.746 $\pm$ 0.044 and 0.705 $\pm$ 0.093 (Figure~\ref{fig:roc}). On the surface these numbers look similar, but the confusion matrices (Figure~\ref{fig:confusion}) tell a more informative story. At the 100\% threshold, where mixed stones form the majority (65\%), the model correctly identifies 90\% of mixed stones while missing the majority of pure stones (44\% recall). When the threshold is relaxed to 90\%, the class balance inverts to 63\% pure and the bias flips: 77\% recall on pure, 58\% on mixed.
 
This pattern is not a model failure---it is a systematic consequence of moderate class imbalance. The model responds to the shift in prior by leaning toward whichever class dominates, rather than learning a truly threshold-invariant visual feature for purity. The substantially higher fold-to-fold variance at the 90\% threshold (AUC std = 0.093 vs. 0.044) is also telling: near-boundary stones---those with 89--91\% of a single mineral---look virtually identical regardless of which side of the cutoff they fall on, introducing label noise that destabilises training.
 
From a clinical standpoint, the 100\% threshold is the more defensible choice. The urologists at Amsterdam UMC consider any detectable mixture clinically relevant to the lithogenic process \cite{Daudon2014}, and the tighter definition produces a cleaner training signal. At neither threshold does Model A reach the precision--recall balance needed for reliable standalone deployment, but its AUC of 0.715 demonstrates that genuine visual information about stone purity exists in ex vivo photographs and is recoverable from moderate-scale training data.
 
\subsection*{Compositional regression captures real structure}
 
The central question of this thesis was whether a model can predict continuous FTIR composition from a photograph. Model B's results provide an encouraging answer. A Spearman correlation of 0.72 for CaOx means the model correctly orders stones along the calcium oxalate fraction axis in roughly three out of four pairwise comparisons. For CaP ($\rho$ = 0.56) and the three minority classes ($\rho$ = 0.45--0.48), the ordering signal is weaker but clearly above chance. A naive predictor that always outputs the dataset mean composition would achieve $\rho$ = 0 by construction; a model predicting the training-set mean for each dominant class would achieve slightly positive $\rho$ but no within-class ordering. Model B's correlations are not trivially explainable by class membership alone.
 
The absolute errors are larger than would be needed for clinical use. An MAE of 0.27 for CaOx corresponds to predictions being off by roughly 27 percentage points on average, which cannot reliably distinguish a 60\% CaOx stone from a 90\% stone. This is the honest current limitation of the approach. However, it should be read alongside the dataset constraints: 414 stones is small by deep learning standards, and much of the training signal is concentrated in the two dominant classes. The error level is consistent with what Ruf et al.\ observed in the methodologically analogous task of predicting mineral fractions from geological drill core images at comparable dataset sizes \cite{Ruf2024}; their study also showed that per-mineral MAE decreases substantially as training data grows.
 
The per-class error distributions reveal an important structural pattern (Figure~\ref{fig:violin}). CaOx predictions are biased downward while CaP predictions are biased upward. These two classes co-occur in over half the stones in our dataset, and the softmax simplex constraint means that overestimating one necessarily depresses the other. What the model appears to learn is a reasonable dominant-class signal for CaOx and CaP separately, but it has insufficient training examples of intermediate mixed compositions---say, 40\% CaOx and 40\% CaP---to partition the two fractions accurately when both are large. A similar inter-mineral confusion between co-occurring classes was reported by Ruf et al.\ \cite{Ruf2024}, and by the Ochoa-Ruiz group for in vivo images where COM and CHP co-occur \cite{LopezTiro2024}. In clinical terms, this CaOx--CaP confusion has real consequences: Daudon has argued that even minor CaP content can indicate specific metabolic dysfunctions such as tubular acidification defects, where the difference between 10\% and 40\% secondary CaP carries distinct diagnostic weight \cite{Daudon2014}.
 
For the rarer classes, the model achieves lower MAE values (UA = 0.137, MAP = 0.052, CYS = 0.099) partly because most stones contain near-zero fractions of these minerals, leaving less room for large absolute errors. The moderate correlations suggest the model captures enough visual signal to rank stones within these classes, though the test set contains too few MAP-dominant stones ($\sim$2) to interpret dominant-class F1 for that class.
 
\subsection*{Regression matches classification, and sometimes beats it}
 
Comparing Model B and Model C on the shared task of dominant class prediction directly addresses the second sub-question. Both achieved macro F1 $\approx$ 0.45 on the test set, roughly 3.5 times the random-label baseline of 0.13. The key question is not just whether they are equal on average, but whether the regression framing trades anything meaningful.
 
For CaOx, the two models are nearly indistinguishable (F1 $\approx$ 0.70). For CaP, Model B clearly outperforms Model C (F1 $\approx$ 0.58 vs. $\approx$ 0.38). This is a substantive result: by optimising the full composition vector rather than a categorical label, Model B develops a richer internal representation of CaP stones that transfers to the dominant-class task. Learning \textit{how much} CaP is present---even when CaOx is the dominant class---helps the model correctly identify stones where CaP takes over as the majority component. Model C, which only ever sees the dominant label as a target, has no mechanism to learn this.
 
The trade-off is on rare classes. Model C outperforms Model B on CYS (F1 $\approx$ 0.57 vs. $\approx$ 0.00) and MAP, because its class-weighted cross-entropy loss explicitly compensates for the imbalance by upweighting minority classes during training. Model B's KL divergence loss optimises overall composition accuracy, which is dominated by CaOx and CaP. A weighted KL formulation---penalising errors on minority classes more heavily---is the natural fix, and the infrastructure for it already exists in our codebase. Based on these results, we expect weighted KL to substantially recover rare-class performance without sacrificing composition accuracy on the common classes.
 
The net picture is that Model B offers everything Model C offers for majority classes, plus a continuous composition output that carries additional information beyond the dominant label---all at the same classification cost. This confirms the practical motivation for the regression framing.
 
\subsection*{Clinical relevance and context}
 
The clinical motivation for this work is that stone composition is not a categorical variable, yet the existing approaches treat it as one. Daudon showed that FTIR-based composition percentages contain clinically relevant information beside the dominant class: the ratio of COM to COD within the calcium oxalate fraction indicates oxaluria severity, and the proportion of CaP sub-types reflects tubular acidification status \cite{Daudon2014}.
 
The current accuracy is not sufficient for clinical deployment. However, the approach has value as a screening tool: a model predicting that a stone is 70\% CaOx with 25\% CaP secondary provides more information than a label of ``calcium oxalate stone,'' even if the fractions are imprecise. Real-time approximate composition estimates during ureteroscopy---before the stone is retrieved for FTIR---could guide intraoperative decisions on fragmentation technique and sampling. The fact that Model B can match a dedicated classifier on dominant-class prediction while simultaneously providing these estimates suggests the regression framing is not just theoretically appealing but practically viable, at this dataset size and without architectural complexity beyond a standard ResNet18.
 
\subsection*{Comparison to prior work}
 
No prior study has attempted continuous composition prediction from kidney stone photographs, so direct quantitative comparison with the literature is not possible. Our Model C classification results provide a conventional benchmark on the same data.
 
Kim et al.\ reported per-class recall values of 0.50--0.97 for deep learning classification of ex vivo digital photographs across six mineral classes \cite{Kim2022}. Black et al.\ achieved 74\% overall accuracy on a smaller ex vivo dataset \cite{Black2020}. The Ochoa-Ruiz group reported macro accuracies between 65\% and 97\% depending on class count and imaging conditions, using ResNet50 on in vivo endoscopic images \cite{LopezTiro2021, LopezTiro2024}. Veltheer reported macro F1 values in a comparable range using two-step transfer learning on the same MOCKI dataset with a larger ResNet50 backbone \cite{veltheer2025}. Our Model C macro F1 of 0.43--0.47 falls within this range, though comparisons across these studies are confounded by different class definitions, imaging protocols, dataset sizes, and evaluation metrics.
 
The closest methodological parallel for our regression approach is Ruf et al., who predicted continuous mineral fractions from drill core images using a pretrained CNN, on a dataset of comparable size ($\sim$361 data points) \cite{Ruf2024}. Their findings---that co-occurring minerals with similar visual textures are hardest to separate, and that overall MAE is consistent with our range---independently validate the feasibility of the compositional regression formulation in image-based mineral analysis, despite the domain difference.
 
\subsection*{Limitations}
 
Several limitations should be noted. The dataset, while larger than many prior kidney stone imaging studies, remains small by the standards of deep learning: 414 stones limits what can be learned about minority classes and intermediate mixed compositions. MAP has only 12 dominant-class stones in the full dataset, and even CYS has 39; the test sets for these classes are too small for stable metric estimates.
 
All images were acquired ex vivo under controlled conditions at a single institution. In vivo endoscopic images present substantially different challenges---partial stone visibility, variable lighting, blood and tissue in the field---and our results do not directly generalise to that setting. Single-institution data also limits the generalisability of the composition distribution; Amsterdam UMC's patient population may not reflect the prevalence of different stone types in other healthcare settings.
 
We did not perform extensive hyperparameter optimisation beyond the auxiliary loss weight sweep. In particular, we did not ablate the backbone architecture, the augmentation strategy, or the choice of KL divergence versus MAE as the primary regression loss. Given the tight deadline constraints, these were left as open questions for future work. Finally, we have no outcome data linking our composition predictions to clinical decisions or patient outcomes, which would ultimately be needed to demonstrate translational utility.
 
\subsection*{Future directions}
 
The most impactful extension of this work would be to in vivo endoscopic images, where real-time composition estimation could directly inform treatment during ureteroscopy. The two-step transfer learning strategy developed in this group \cite{veltheer2025}---pretraining on ex vivo images before fine-tuning on in vivo data---provides a natural path for that extension, and our composition model could serve as the ex vivo pretrain stage.
 
On the modelling side, two improvements are within reach without additional data. First, applying inverse-frequency weighting to the KL divergence loss would address the rare-class weakness identified in the B vs. C comparison; the implementation is already in our codebase and requires only a configuration change. Second, a hybrid loss that combines weighted KL for composition regression with class-weighted cross-entropy for dominant-class prediction could recover rare-class F1 while preserving the CaP advantage of the regression framing.
 
Beyond model improvements, the most valuable investment would be a larger and more compositionally diverse dataset. The CaOx--CaP confusion we observe is a structural consequence of the model seeing too few examples of intermediate mixed compositions; more stones with 30--60\% fractions of both classes would directly address this. Multi-institution data collection, potentially coordinated through the networks established by the Ochoa-Ruiz and Daudon groups, would also enable the external validation that single-institution studies cannot provide.

\section*{Conclusion}
 
This thesis asked whether a deep learning model can predict the continuous mineral composition of a kidney stone from its ex vivo photograph. The answer is yes, in a meaningful but bounded sense. Model B learns to rank mineral fractions with Spearman correlations of 0.72 for calcium oxalate and 0.56 for calcium phosphate, and achieves dominant-class macro F1 on par with a dedicated multiclass classifier despite never being trained on categorical labels. This demonstrates that FTIR-calibrated compositional information is recoverable from photographic appearance, and that framing the problem as regression rather than classification is not only theoretically sound but practically viable.
 
Addressing the first sub-question, Model A distinguishes pure from mixed stones with AUC values of 0.715 (100\% threshold) and 0.703 (90\% threshold), confirming that a genuine purity signal exists in the visual features of ex vivo photographs. Performance is constrained by class imbalance, with the model biased toward the majority class at both thresholds. The 100\% purity definition produces more stable cross-validation results and is more clinically defensible, as any detectable mixture is considered relevant for treatment decisions.
 
Addressing the second sub-question, the compositional regression model matches the multiclass classifier on overall dominant-class macro F1 (0.483 vs. 0.430) and outperforms it on calcium phosphate identification (F1 $\approx$ 0.58 vs. $\approx$ 0.38). The classifier holds an advantage on rare classes (CYS, MAP) due to its class-weighted loss, a known limitation of the KL divergence objective that is addressable without architectural changes. Together, these results show that the regression framing is no worse than direct classification for the majority classes, and better for calcium phosphate, while also providing the continuous composition output that motivates the approach.
 
The primary bottleneck is dataset size. With approximately 420 stones, rare-class learning is limited and the model sees too few intermediate mixed compositions to accurately partition co-occurring minerals. Extending this work to larger, multi-institutional datasets and ultimately to in vivo endoscopic images are the most important next steps toward clinical translation.
