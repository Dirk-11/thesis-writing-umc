\begin{abstract}
Kidney stone composition determines treatment strategy and recurrence risk, yet other approaches assign stones to discrete mineral categories, discarding the continuous compositional information measured by Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy. This information is still important as the proportions of mixed stones matters regarding the cause and dietary advice. We present a deep learning study to predict continuous FTIR-calibrated mineral composition percentages directly from ex vivo kidney stone photographs.
 
Three models were trained on 414 stones: a binary pure/mixed classifier (Model A), a compositional regression model predicting five-class mineral fractions using KL divergence loss with an auxiliary presence detection head (Model B), and a multiclass classifier serving as a controlled baseline (Model C). All models share a ResNet18 backbone with two-phase staged fine-tuning and are evaluated under five-fold cross-validation and a held-out test set.
 
Model A distinguished pure from mixed stones with AUC = 0.715 on the test set at the 100\% purity threshold, with performance biased toward the majority class at both thresholds. Model B predicted calcium oxalate and calcium phosphate fractions with Spearman correlations of 0.67 and 0.51, respectively. It achieved a dominant-class macro F1 of 0.483, comparable to Model C (0.430) and it also predicts the full composition of the stone. Model B outperformed Model C on calcium phosphate (F1 = 0.44 vs. 0.38) and uric acid (F1 = 0.46 vs. 0.35), and showed a smaller advantage on cystine (F1 = 0.59 vs. 0.55). This suggests that learning the full composition teaches the model something more useful than just the main mineral.

These results show that continuous mineral composition is learnable from stone photographs, and that the regression framing is competitive with direct classification. Current accuracy is not yet sufficient for clinical deployment, but the approach provides a foundation for future work with larger datasets and an extension to in vivo endoscopic imaging.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}

\subsection*{Kidney stone disease and the importance of composition}

Kidney stone disease is one of the most common urological conditions worldwide, with incidence rates ranging from 1\% in parts of Asia to 13\% in North America and a global trend of rising prevalence over the past three decades \cite{Türk2022, Qian2022, Sorokin2017, Stamatelou2023}. In 2021 alone, an estimated 106 million new cases were recorded globally \cite{Qian2022}. Up to 50\% for patients experience stone recurrence within 10 years of their first episode.

Preventing recurrence requires knowing what the stone is made of. Different mineral compositions reflect different underlying metabolic disorders, and treatment decisions---dietary modification, urine alkalinisation, or pharmacotherapy---depend directly on this information \cite{Daudon2016, Corrales2021}. The reference method for compositional analysis is Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR), which can accurately identify the chemical nature and relative proportions of each mineral component \cite{Basiri2025, Daudon2016}. However, FTIR is performed ex vivo after the stone has been retrieved and the results take days to weeks to reach the clinician. It would save a lot of time if this could be done during the surgery.

Another option is to estimate composition based on the stone’s visual characteristics. Well-trained clinicians can deduce its composition from surface texture and color. This is an approach called endoscopic stone recognition (ESR). formalized in the Daudon morpho-constitutional classification system \cite{Daudon2016, Corrales2021}. In practice, however, ESR requires years of experience and shows poor agreement between observers \cite{Randall2023}, which makes it unreliable.

\subsection*{Using deep learning for classification}

This is where deep learning becomes relevant. Multiple groups have shown that convolutional neural networks (CNNs) can classify kidney stone types from photographs with reasonable accuracy. Most of this work has focused on in vivo endoscopic images: the group of Ochoa-Ruiz and Daul explored CNN-based classification, two-step transfer learning, and metric learning on ureteroscopic images \cite{LopezTiro2021, LopezTiro2024, GonzalezZapata2024}, while Estrade demonstrated recognition of pure and mixed stone morphologies from both still images and video \cite{Estrade2022, Estrade2022video}. Other groups have worked with ex vivo photographs taken after stone extraction \cite{Black2020}. Kim et al. classified ex vivo stone photographs by dominant composition type \cite{Kim2022}, and Serrat et al. built a classification model for retrieved stones \cite{Serrat2017}. Zhu et al. developed models using intraoperative images to predict composition in real time \cite{Zhu2024}, Leng et al. combined segmentation and classification from endoscopic video \cite{Leng2024}, and Onal and Tekgul explored smartphone-based microscopy \cite{Onal2022}. A comprehensive benchmark of these approaches was provided by El Beze et al. \cite{ElBeze2022}.

\paragraph{}

Despite this progress, these methods approach the problem as a classification task. The model classifies each stone to a mineral category or to a predefined mixture category from the Daudon system, and then produces a discrete label as output. This method ignores continuous mineralogical composition measured by FTIR.

Most kidney stones are mixtures of two or more minerals, and the relative proportions matter clinically \cite{Daudon2016}. A stone that is 90\% calcium oxalate monohydrate (COM) with 10\% calcium phosphate (CaP) suggests a different metabolic profile than one that is 60\% COM and 40\% CaP, yet both would receive the same label under these existing classification models. To our knowledge, no prior study has attempted to predict continuous FTIR-calibrated mineral composition percentages directly from stone photographs.

\subsection*{Research question}

The central question of this thesis is: can a deep learning model predict the continuous mineral composition of a kidney stone from its ex vivo photograph, as calibrated by FTIR spectroscopy?

We address this through two sub-questions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item How accurately can a binary classifier distinguish pure from mixed stones based on visual features alone?
    \item How well does a compositional regression model predict the dominant mineral class compared to a conventional multiclass classifier trained on the same data?
\end{enumerate}

We work with ex vivo photographs, taken after stone retrieval, outside the body as a deliberate first step before tackling the harder in vivo endoscopic setting. Prior work from this group showed the feasibility of deep learning classification of kidney stones \cite{veltheer2025}. This study is the first to apply deep learning to an ex-vivo dataset created by Amsterdam AMC.

\section*{Methods}

\subsection*{Study design}

We trained and compared three deep learning models, each addressing a different aspect of the composition profiling problem (Figure~\ref{fig:workflow}):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Model A}: a binary classifier that distinguishes pure from mixed stones,
    \item \textbf{Model B}: a compositional regression model that predicts continuous mineral percentages,
    \item \textbf{Model C}: a multiclass classifier that predicts the dominant mineral class.
\end{itemize}

Model A addresses the most basic clinical question: does this stone contain a mixture of minerals? We wanted to start with the simpler task and see how well a model can tell pure stones from mixed ones. Model B is the central contribution of this thesis, an attempt to predict continuous FTIR-calibrated composition from photographs rather than a categorical label. Model C is there as a comparison for Model B. Both can be evaluated on dominant-class prediction, which separates the effect of the regression framing from the architecture and training procedure.

All three models share the same backbone and training procedure and differ only in their output head and loss function. The full pipeline is publicly available at \url{https://github.com/Dirkdeboer/umc_thesis}.

\subsection*{Dataset}

The dataset consists of 416 kidney stone fragments collected at Amsterdam UMC (VUmc) as part of the MOCKI study. Each fragment was photographed ex vivo from three sides, giving three photographs per stone. Two stones were missing FTIR labels, which left 414 stones (1242 images) to work with.

Mineral composition was determined by FTIR spectroscopy, which outputs a top ten of the most likely continuous percentage for each component present in the stone. The most confident composition is used as the ground truth in this case.

\subsection*{Pre-processing}

The raw clinical data needed four cleaning steps before it could be used for modelling. Fragment identifiers were forward-filled across photo rows, since only the first photo of each stone carried the identifier in the original spreadsheet. Known data-entry errors in the percentage columns were corrected against clinical verification (six stones). Photo identifiers with formatting inconsistencies, such as missing prefixes or single-letter abbreviations, were standardized, and trailing whitespace in the component labels was stripped.

\subsubsection*{Class consolidation}

The raw FTIR data distinguishes nine mineral classes, but reliably separating all nine from photographs of only 414 stones would leave too few training examples per class. Prior automated kidney stone recognition studies have consistently worked with 4--6 target classes \cite{Black2020, Kim2022, LopezTiro2021, veltheer2025}. In consultation with the urologists at Amsterdam UMC, we therefore consolidated the nine raw FTIR classes into six target classes through a two-stage remapping (Figure~\ref{fig:remapping}). In the first stage, we examined stones labelled as ``OTH'' (other) using the FTIR sub-identification field. Where the mineral could be chemically identified, we reassigned it to the appropriate class: urate salts and uric acid dihydrate (30 stones) were assigned to UA, calcium-magnesium phosphate (4 stones) to CaP, and newberyite (1 stone) to MAP. The remaining OTH stones---calcite, protein, and xanthine---stayed as OTH because they have no clear chemical relationship to the major classes. In the second stage, we merged subtypes into final classes: COM and COD into CaOx, CHP, CHPD, and CMP into CaP, with UA, MAP, and CYS remaining as individual classes.

For Models B and C, the OTH class was excluded in two steps, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:remapping}. First, the 4 stones with OTH as their dominant component were removed entirely, as the class is chemically heterogeneous and too small to train or evaluate reliably. Second, for the 32 stones where OTH appeared only as a secondary or tertiary component, the OTH fraction was dropped from the composition vector and the remaining five-class fractions were renormalized to sum to 1. This means the regression targets for these stones do not represent the OTH component; the fraction is absorbed into the dominant mineral. For 30 of these 32 stones the OTH fraction is 0.2, so the distortion is modest; the remaining two carry an OTH fraction of 0.4. After applying both steps, 410 stones distributed across 5 target classes remain.

\subsubsection*{Composition vectors and purity labels}

For each stone, we built a composition vector by summing the FTIR percentages from all positions (primary, secondary, tertiary) into the matching class bucket, then normalising the vector to sum to 1.0. This normalized vector is the regression target for Model B, and its argmax gives the dominant class label for Model C.

Stones whose composition was 100\% a single mineral were labelled \textit{pure}; the rest were labelled \textit{mixed}. We chose this strict threshold in consultation with the urologists at Amsterdam UMC, who treat any detectable mixture as clinically relevant. Prior work has used a looser threshold of 90\% \cite{Kim2022}, so we evaluate Model A at both thresholds and compare the effect on classification performance.

\subsubsection*{Data splitting}

All splits were done at the stone level to prevent data leakage, so the three photographs of a stone always land in the same split. For each model we held out 20\% of stones as a test set (stratified sampling), then split the remaining 80\% into five stratified folds for cross-validation. The stratification variable matched each model's target: the binary label for Model A, and the dominant mineral class for Models B and C.

Because Models B and C use the same 410-stone pool, the same stratification variable, and the same random seed, their fold assignments and test sets are identical. This lets us compare them fold by fold rather than only on the aggregated test set. The held-out test set was used only for the final evaluation reported here.

\subsection*{Image preprocessing and augmentation}

Images were resized to $224 \times 224$ pixels as this is the expected input for the pre-trained model. Before normalizing, we applied a background masking step where pixels with a mean RGB intensity below 20/255 were set to zero. This keeps the model from learning artifacts in the black background instead of the stone surface. After masking, pixel values were normalized using ImageNet channel statistics.

During training, we used data augmentation to increase the effective dataset size: random horizontal and vertical flips (each with probability 0.5), random rotation up to 45 degrees, and color jitter (brightness and contrast $\pm 0.4$, saturation $\pm 0.2$). Because the stones are photographed ex vivo on a flat surface, their orientation is arbitrary, which is why we use such aggressive geometric augmentation. The color jitter accounts for the lighting variation between imaging sessions.

\subsection*{Architecture of the model}

All three models use ResNet18 \cite{He2016} pretrained on ImageNet as their backbone. ResNet18 is an 18-layer residual network whose skip connections allow stable training of deep networks. We chose it over the larger ResNet50 used in most prior kidney stone studies \cite{LopezTiro2024, veltheer2025}, because with only 414 stones (410 for the composition models) the larger model tends to overfit. This is a known limitation of high-capacity models on small medical imaging datasets \cite{Raghu2019}, and we confirmed it on our own data (Appendix~\ref{app:resnet50}). The backbone's original classification head is removed, which leaves a 512-dimensional feature vector from the global average pooling layer. Each model attaches its own task-specific output head to this vector.

\subsubsection*{Model A: binary classifier}

Model A adds a dropout layer and a single linear layer that maps the feature vector to two outputs (pure vs.\ mixed), trained with weighted cross-entropy loss. At the 100\% purity threshold the classes are imbalanced, with mixed stones making up 65\% of the dataset, so we applied inverse-frequency class weighting to the loss. Each class weight was set proportional to the inverse of its frequency in the training fold, which upweights pure stones to make up for their underrepresentation. At inference time, the predicted probability of the mixed class is averaged across the three photos of a stone and a threshold of 0.5 is applied.

\subsubsection*{Model B: compositional regression}

Model B is the central model of this thesis. It predicts a continuous five-dimensional composition vector, where each element is the estimated fraction of a mineral class (CaOx, CaP, UA, MAP, CYS). The architecture has two output heads sharing the same backbone.

The composition head maps the feature vector through a hidden layer (512 $\to$ 128 units with ReLU and dropout) to five outputs, which then pass through a softmax:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{c}_k = \frac{\exp(z_k)}{\sum_{j=1}^{5} \exp(z_j)}, \quad k = 1, \ldots, 5
\end{equation}
This makes sure the compositional prediction sums to 1, making them comparable to the FTIR composition vectors. The softmax is used purely as a normalization constraint rather than as a classifier.
The composition head is trained with the Kullback--Leibler (KL) divergence between the predicted and true composition:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{KL}} = \sum_{k=1}^{5} c_k \log \frac{c_k}{\hat{c}_k}
\end{equation}
Composition targets with a true fraction of zero use the conventional $0 \log 0 = 0$ definition, and the softmax output is bounded away from zero by construction, so the loss stays numerically stable. We chose KL divergence over simpler options like mean absolute error because it is the natural loss for probability distributions on the simplex. In practice it penalises confidently wrong predictions more heavily than small errors in already-uncertain classes, which is what we want when the model has to get the dominant component right while still tracking the minority fractions. Each per-class KL term is also scaled by an inverse-frequency weight from the training pool, which upweights the rare classes (MAP, CYS, UA) relative to the dominant CaOx class.

The auxiliary presence head has the same architecture but outputs independent sigmoid activations, one per class, predicting whether each mineral is present in the stone. It is trained with binary cross-entropy loss and gives an extra training signal that pushes the backbone to learn features sensitive to minority components, which might otherwise be ignored because their fractions are small. The total loss combines the two terms with a weight of 0.3 on the auxiliary loss. We selected this value from \{0.0, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0\} based on validation MAE and macro F1 (Appendix~\ref{app:lambda}).

At inference time, the composition predictions from the three photos of a stone are averaged and re-normalized to sum to 1.

\subsubsection*{Model C: multiclass classifier}

Model C has the same architecture as Model A (dropout + linear) but outputs five logits, one per mineral class. The target is the dominant component in the FTIR composition vector. The dataset is imbalanced here too, with CaOx at 48\% of stones and MAP under 3\%, so we again used inverse-frequency class weighting in the cross-entropy loss to stop the model from simply predicting the majority class.

Model C is the comparison point for Model B. Both can be evaluated on dominant-class prediction. The class probabilities from the three photos are averaged and the class with the highest mean probability is selected.

\subsection*{Training procedure}

All models were trained with a two-phase staged fine-tuning strategy, following prior kidney stone classification work \cite{LopezTiro2024, veltheer2025}. In the first phase we kept the backbone fixed and trained only the output head for 5 epochs, which lets the head learn a reasonable mapping from the ImageNet features before the backbone weights change. In the second phase we unfroze the whole network and trained end-to-end, with a much lower learning rate for the backbone than for the head. The idea is that the pretrained features are still valuable and should be adjusted slightly instead of being completely replaced. Training ran for up to 20 epochs (Model A) or 25 epochs (Models B and C), with early stopping on validation loss. All hyperparameters are listed in Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams}.

\subsection*{Evaluation}

All metrics are computed at the stone level after combining the predictions across the three photos.

For Model A, we report AUC-ROC as the main metric. For Model B, we apply top-3 post-processing to the raw softmax output before computing metrics. The two smallest predicted fractions per stone are set to zero and the rest are renormalized to sum to one, which reflects the fact that every stone in our dataset contains at most three mineral components. We then report MAE and Spearman's $\rho$ between the post-processed predictions and the FTIR ground truth, both per class and overall. To compare Model B directly against Model C, we also derive a dominant-class prediction from Model B by taking the class with the highest predicted fraction, and compute macro-F1 and per-class F1 for both models on this shared task. For Model C, we report macro-F1 and per-class F1.

For each model, we also report a random-label baseline. Here an identical model is trained on data where the stone labels have been randomly permuted at the stone level, so all three photographs of a stone get the same shuffled label. This keeps the within-stone consistency but destroys any link between visual features and composition. The baseline is then trained and evaluated exactly like the main model. Comparing against it confirms that the trained model's performance comes from real visual learning rather than distributional artifacts or data leakage.

All code, configuration files, and training scripts are available at \url{https://github.com/Dirkdeboer/umc_thesis}. The image data and FTIR labels are held at Amsterdam UMC and are not publicly available, but may be accessible on request for academic research.

\section*{Results}

\subsection*{Dataset}

After preprocessing and class merging, the dataset comprised 414 stones with valid composition vectors across six mineral classes including OTH (Figure~\ref{fig:presence}). The four OTH-dominant stones are dropped for the composition models (Models B and C), which leaves five target classes across 410 stones. CaOx was the dominant mineral in 199 stones (48\%) and CaP in 121 (29\%), so the two together account for 77\% of dominant-class assignments. UA, MAP, CYS, and OTH each appeared as the primary component in fewer than 10\% of stones. CaP also turned up as a secondary or tertiary component in 109 stones (26\%), against only 39 stones (9\%) for CaOx, which shows how often CaP sits alongside another mineral in a mixed stone.

At the 100\% purity threshold, 146 stones (35\%) were labelled pure and 268 (65\%) mixed. For the 90\% threshold, this shifts to 229 pure (55\%) and 185 mixed (45\%), which shows how much the class balance depends on where the threshold is set.

\subsection*{Model A: binary classification of pure vs. mixed stones}

Model A reached an AUC of $0.750 \pm 0.026$ on cross-validation at the 100\% purity threshold, against $0.719 \pm 0.110$ at the 90\% threshold (Figure~\ref{fig:roc}). Both clearly beat the random-label baseline (AUC $= 0.490 \pm 0.080$), so the model is picking up real visual features related to purity. On the held-out test set, AUC was 0.715 at the 100\% threshold and 0.723 at 90\%. The 90\% threshold edges ahead on the test set, but that fits the much wider fold-to-fold variance there (std $= 0.110$ vs.\ $0.026$), which makes a single held-out estimate noisier.

The 100\% threshold did slightly better and varied less across folds. This may seem backwards, since a stricter cutoff gives fewer pure stones, but it also makes the distinction cleaner. A stone counts as pure only if it has no secondary minerals at all, which gives the model a sharper signal to learn. At 90\% the line between pure and mixed is blurrier, and stones near the cutoff look much the same on either side.

Even with inverse-frequency class weighting, the model still leans toward the majority class at both thresholds (Figure~\ref{fig:confusion}). At 100\%, recall on mixed stones stays higher than on pure stones (79\% vs.\ 44\%); at 90\% the pattern flips (77\% pure recall vs.\ 58\% mixed). This comes down to the visual difficulty near the boundary. Stones that are borderline pure or mixed look nearly identical whichever side they fall on, and class weighting can move the decision boundary but cannot recover information that is not in the image.

\subsection*{Model B: compositional regression}
The prediction performance of model B varied a lot by class (Figure~\ref{fig:violin}). On the held-out test set, CaOx was the strongest (MAE $= 0.295$, $\rho = 0.67$), followed by CaP (MAE $= 0.260$, $\rho = 0.51$). These are the two classes with the most training signal, together making up 77\% of dominant-class assignments (CaOx 48\%, CaP 29\%) and covering the widest range of true fractions. UA, MAP, and CYS had lower MAE values (0.169, 0.078, and 0.107), partly because their true fraction is zero in any stone where the mineral is absent, which leaves less room for large errors. Their rank correlations were still moderate to strong ($\rho$ between 0.45 and 0.60), so the model orders stones sensibly even for the minority classes. The random-label baseline gave MAE $= 0.432$ for CaOx ($\rho = -0.07$) and MAE $= 0.290$ for CaP ($\rho = 0.13$). This shows that the model's errors and correlations come from real visual learning rather than the shape of the composition distribution.

The violin plot shows an asymmetry in the errors. CaOx predictions skew below zero, so the model tends to underestimate calcium oxalate, while CaP skews the other way and gets overestimated. Because CaOx and CaP co-occur in many mixed stones and the fractions have to sum to one, the model has trouble splitting the composition between the two. For the minority classes (UA, MAP, CYS) the errors are more symmetric and sit close to zero.

\section*{Discussion}
 
This study asked whether continuous mineral composition can be predicted from ex vivo kidney stone photographs, as an alternative to the discrete classification framing used in all prior approaches. The result is a proof of concept, with limitations. Model B learns to rank mineral fractions with Spearman correlations of up to 0.67 for calcium oxalate and 0.51 for calcium phosphate, and reaches a dominant-class macro F1 on par with a dedicated multiclass classifier (0.483 vs.\ 0.430 on the held-out test set). These results establish compositional regression as a viable and informative task, even on a dataset of the size available here.

\subsection*{Binary classification depends on the purity threshold}

Model A achieved AUC values of 0.715 and 0.723 on the held-out test set at the 100\% and 90\% purity thresholds, respectively, with cross-validation AUCs of $0.750 \pm 0.026$ and $0.719 \pm 0.110$ (Figure~\ref{fig:roc}). On the surface these numbers look similar, but the confusion matrices (Figure~\ref{fig:confusion}) tell a more informative story. At the 100\% threshold, where mixed stones make up 65\% of test stones, the model correctly identifies 79\% of mixed stones but only 16 of 36 pure stones (44\% recall). When the threshold is relaxed to 90\%, the class balance inverts to 55\% pure and the bias flips with it, to 77\% recall on pure stones and 58\% on mixed.

This pattern is a systematic consequence of moderate class imbalance. The model leans toward whichever class dominates rather than learning a truly threshold-invariant visual feature for purity. The much higher fold-to-fold variance at the 90\% threshold (AUC std $= 0.110$ vs.\ $0.026$) points the same way. Stones sitting near the cutoff look virtually identical regardless of which side they fall on, which introduces label noise and destabilizes training.

From a clinical standpoint, the 100\% threshold is the more defensible choice. The urologists at Amsterdam UMC consider any detectable mixture clinically relevant, and the tighter definition produces a cleaner training signal. At neither threshold does Model A reach the precision/recall balance needed for reliable standalone deployment, but its AUC of 0.715 shows that genuine visual information about stone purity exists in ex vivo photographs and is recoverable from moderate-scale training data.

\subsection*{Compositional regression captures real structure}

The central question of this thesis was whether a model can predict continuous FTIR composition from a photograph. Model B's results are encouraging. A Spearman correlation of $\rho = 0.67$ for CaOx is a moderate-to-strong positive rank correlation, meaning the model orders stones along the calcium oxalate fraction axis in roughly the right sequence. For CaP ($\rho = 0.51$) and the three minority classes ($\rho$ between 0.45 and 0.60), the ordering signal is weaker but clearly above chance. The random-label baseline achieves $\rho = -0.07$ for CaOx (MAE $= 0.432$) and $\rho = 0.13$ for CaP (MAE $= 0.290$), which confirms that Model B's ordering signal is real and not an artifact of the composition distribution.

Beyond confirming that learning happened, the continuous output carries qualitatively different information from a class label. A classifier can only say that a stone is CaOx-dominant; Model B additionally estimates that it is, say, 70\% CaOx and 25\% CaP. That distinction has clinical weight, and the continuous output is also well suited to tracking composition changes in recurrent stone formers over time, where the question is not which mineral dominates but whether the balance is shifting. Neither use case is available to a classification model.

The absolute errors remain too large for clinical use. An MAE of 0.295 for CaOx means predictions are off by about 30 percentage points on average, enough that the model cannot reliably tell a 60\% CaOx stone from a 90\% one. This is the honest current limitation of the approach. It should be read alongside the dataset constraints, though. With 414 stones, small by deep learning standards, most of the training signal sits in the two dominant classes. The error level is consistent with what Ruf et al.\ found in the analogous task of predicting mineral fractions from geological drill core images at comparable dataset sizes \cite{Ruf2024}, and their study also showed per-mineral MAE dropping substantially as training data grows.

The per-class error distributions reveal a structural pattern worth mentioning (Figure~\ref{fig:violin}). CaOx predictions are biased downward while CaP predictions are biased upward. The two classes co-occur in over half the stones in our dataset, and because the softmax fractions must sum to one, overestimating one necessarily leads to underestimation of the other. The model seems to learn a reasonable dominant-class signal for CaOx and CaP separately, but it has too few examples of intermediate mixed compositions to split the two fractions correctly when both are large. A similar confusion between co-occurring classes was reported by Ruf et al.\ \cite{Ruf2024}, and by the Ochoa-Ruiz group for in vivo images where COM and CHP co-occur \cite{LopezTiro2024}. This CaOx--CaP confusion matters clinically, since Daudon has argued that even minor CaP content can point to specific metabolic dysfunctions such as tubular acidification defects, where the difference between 10\% and 40\% secondary CaP carries real diagnostic weight \cite{Daudon2014}.

For the minority classes, the model achieves lower MAE values (UA $= 0.169$, MAP $= 0.078$, CYS $= 0.107$), partly because stones without these minerals have a true fraction of zero by definition, which leaves less room for large absolute errors. The moderate correlations suggest the model picks up enough visual signal to rank stones within these classes, though the test set holds only 2 MAP-dominant stones, so the dominant-class F1 for MAP should be read as indicative only.
 
\subsection*{Model B matches model C for classification}
 
Comparing Model B and Model C on the shared task of dominant class prediction directly addresses the second sub-question. Both achieved macro F1 above 0.40 on the test set (0.483 and 0.430 for Models B and C, respectively), 4.0 and 3.6 times the random-label baseline (0.120).
 
For CaOx the two models are about even, with Model C marginally ahead (F1 = 0.68 vs. 0.69). On CaP the gap opens up in Model B's favour (F1 = 0.44 vs. 0.38). We think this happens because Model B is trained on the full composition vector rather than a single label, so it builds a more detailed internal picture of CaP-containing stones, and that picture carries over to the dominant-class task. Knowing how much CaP a stone contains, even when CaOx dominates, helps the model spot the cases where CaP tips over into the majority. Model C never sees anything but the dominant label, so it has no way to pick this up.

Model B also comes out slightly ahead on CYS (F1 = 0.59 vs. 0.55). We draw no conclusion from MAP, since the test set has too few dominant stones for the F1 to mean anything. The CYS result is suggestive rather than firm given how few cystine stones we have, but it fits the same explanation: learning the amount of each mineral seems to push the model to represent even the rare components, and that representation then helps with dominant-class prediction.

Taken together, Model B does everything Model C does on the common classes and adds a continuous composition output that holds information the dominant label cannot, at no extra cost in classification performance. This is the practical case for framing the problem as regression.

\subsection*{Clinical relevance}
 
The clinical reason for doing this is that stone composition isn't really categorical, even though existing methods treat it as such. Daudon has shown that the FTIR percentages carry useful information beyond just the dominant mineral. Within the calcium oxalate fraction, the balance of COM to COD reflects how severe the oxaluria is, and the mix of CaP sub-types reflects tubular acidification status \cite{Daudon2014}.
The performance of our models are not good enough to use in the clinic. It could still be useful as a screening tool, though. Telling a clinician that a stone is roughly 70\% CaOx with 25\% CaP says more than just labelling it a "calcium oxalate stone," even when the exact fractions are off. A rough composition estimate during ureteroscopy, before the stone is removed and sent for FTIR, could help with decisions on how to fragment the stone and where to sample it. Model B keeps up with a dedicated classifier on dominant-class prediction and gives the composition estimate on top of that, which is encouraging given the modest dataset and the fact that we used nothing more elaborate than a standard ResNet18.
 
\subsection*{Comparison to prior work}
 
No previous study has tried to predict continuous composition from kidney stone photographs, so there's nothing in the literature we can compare against directly. Our Model C results serve as a conventional classification benchmark on the same data.
Kim et al.\ used multiclass deep learning on ex vivo photographs, sorting 965 stones into four composition groups (pure COM, COM+struvite, COM+COD, and pure UA), with per-class sensitivity of 85--95\% \cite{Kim2022}. Black et al.\ trained ResNet-101 on 63 ex vivo images across five mineral classes (COM, UA, struvite, brushite, cystine) and reported 85\% weighted recall \cite{Black2020}. The Ochoa-Ruiz group identified six stone types from in vivo endoscopic images using two-step transfer learning, pretraining on ex vivo images and fine-tuning on endoscopic ones, and reached weighted accuracy as high as 97\% \cite{LopezTiro2021, LopezTiro2024}. Veltheer took a similar two-step approach on Serrat's ex vivo myStone dataset (six Daudon morphological classes) with a ResNet50 backbone, achieving a soft-voted macro F1 of 0.59 across those six classes \cite{veltheer2025}. None of these numbers is directly comparable to our Model C (macro F1 = 0.430). The studies use different class counts (4--6 against our 5), different datasets, and different imaging modalities. Veltheer's macro F1 is the closest metric to ours but still cannot be compared directly: the six morphological classes on Serrat's dataset differ both in number and definition from our five FTIR chemical classes on MOCKI---HAP, STR, and BRU do not map cleanly to our CaP and MAP.
The nearest methodological parallel is outside urology altogether. Ruf et al.\ predicted continuous mineral fractions from geological drill core images with a pretrained CNN, working from 361 labelled image segments \cite{Ruf2024}. They found that co-occurring minerals with similar textures are the hardest to tell apart, and their overall MAE sits in the same range as ours. Both points are reassuring: they suggest the compositional regression idea holds up in image-based mineral analysis generally, not just in our particular setting.
 
\subsection*{Limitations}
 
A couple limitations are worth mentioning. Firstly, the dataset is small by deep learning standards. With only 414 stones, there is a limit to how well the model can learn about the composition of these stones. MAP appears as the dominant mineral in just 12 stones across the whole dataset and CYS in 39, so the test sets for these classes are too small to give stable metrics.
All the images were taken ex vivo under controlled conditions at a single hospital. In vivo endoscopic images are a much harder problem, with the stone only partly visible, lighting that varies, and blood and tissue in the way, so our results won't carry over to that setting without further work. Using data from one institution also limits how far the composition distribution generalizes, since the patient population and current size of our dataset at Amsterdam UMC may not match the mix of stone types seen elsewhere.
As training took three to four hours for each model, there was limited time available for tuning. Besides the sweep over the auxiliary loss weight, we did not test alternative backbones, augmentation strategies, or MAE in place of KL divergence as the main regression loss. These are left for future work. Finally, we have no data connecting our composition predictions to actual clinical decisions or patient outcomes, which is also something you would need to show this approach is useful in practice.
 
\subsection*{Future directions}
 
The most valuable next step would be to move to in vivo endoscopic images, where estimating composition in real time could feed directly into treatment decisions during ureteroscopy. The two-step transfer learning strategy from earlier work in this group \cite{veltheer2025}, pretraining on ex vivo images and then fine-tuning on in vivo data, is a natural route, and our composition model could act as the ex vivo pretraining stage.
On the modelling side, three things could be tried before collecting more data. The first is the loss function. The KL divergence already uses inverse-frequency class weighting, but future work could try heavier weighting or a focal-loss variant that pushes down the easy majority-class predictions further, which might help the rare classes. The second is a hybrid loss that pairs the weighted KL for composition regression with an auxiliary dominant-class head, reinforcing the rare-class signal while keeping the full composition output. The third is the top-3 sparsity step. We currently apply it after inference, but it could be built into training instead. Since most kidney stones contain at most three minerals, enforcing that during training, either with a sparsity penalty on the composition output or by using the presence head to mask fractions in the forward pass, could cut down on implausible multi-component predictions and improve calibration for the minority classes.
The biggest single investment, though, would be a larger and more varied dataset. The CaOx--CaP confusion we see comes down to the model rarely encountering intermediate mixtures, so adding more stones in the 30--60\% range for both minerals would target the problem directly. Collecting data across several institutions would also open up the kind of external validation that a single-site study can't offer.

\section*{Conclusion}
 
This thesis set out to test whether a deep learning model can predict the continuous mineral composition of a kidney stone from an ex vivo photograph. Model B learns to rank mineral fractions with Spearman correlations of 0.67 for calcium oxalate and 0.51 for calcium phosphate, and reaches a dominant-class macro F1 close to a dedicated multiclass classifier even though it is never trained on class labels. This shows that the FTIR composition information is recoverable from how a stone looks, and that treating the problem as regression rather than classification is both sound in principle and workable in practice.
On the first sub-question, Model A tells pure stones from mixed ones with AUC values of 0.715 at the 100\% threshold and 0.723 at 90\%, which confirms that a real purity signal exists in the visual features of ex vivo photographs. Class imbalance is a limiting factor, because the model leans toward the majority class at both thresholds. The 100\% definition gives more stable cross-validation results and is easier to defend clinically, since any detectable mixture matters for treatment.
On the second sub-question, the regression model matches the multiclass classifier on overall dominant-class macro F1 (0.483 vs. 0.430) and does better on calcium phosphate (0.44 vs. 0.38) and uric acid (0.46 vs. 0.35). Its edge on cystine is smaller (0.59 vs. 0.55), and the MAP test set is too small to compare. Taken together, the regression framing is at least as good as direct classification for predicting the dominant mineral, better on calcium phosphate and uric acid, and it gives the continuous composition output that was the point of the approach in the first place.
The main bottleneck is dataset size. With 414 stones, and 410 for the composition models, there isn't enough to learn the rare classes well, and the model sees too few intermediate mixtures to separate co-occurring minerals cleanly. The best ways to move ahead are to collect bigger datasets from many hospitals and, later on, to use in-vivo material taken during real-life endoscopic procedures.
