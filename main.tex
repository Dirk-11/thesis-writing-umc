\begin{abstract}
Kidney stone composition determines treatment strategy and recurrence risk, yet all existing automated approaches assign stones to discrete mineral categories, discarding the continuous compositional information measured by Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy. We present the first deep learning study to predict continuous FTIR-calibrated mineral composition percentages directly from ex vivo kidney stone photographs.
 
We trained three models on 414 stones from the MOCKI dataset (Amsterdam UMC): a binary pure/mixed classifier (Model A), a compositional regression model predicting continuous five-class mineral fractions using KL divergence loss with an auxiliary presence detection head (Model B), and a multiclass dominant-class classifier serving as a controlled baseline (Model C). All models share a ResNet18 backbone with two-phase staged fine-tuning and are evaluated under five-fold cross-validation and a held-out test set.
 
Model A distinguished pure from mixed stones with AUC = 0.715 on the test set at the 100\% purity threshold, with performance biased toward the majority class at both thresholds evaluated. Model B predicted calcium oxalate and calcium phosphate fractions with Spearman correlations of 0.67 and 0.51, respectively. It achieved dominant-class macro F1 of 0.483---comparable to Model C (0.430)---while additionally providing continuous composition estimates. Model B outperformed Model C on calcium phosphate (F1 = 0.44 vs. 0.38) and uric acid (F1 = 0.46 vs. 0.35), and showed a smaller advantage on cystine (F1 = 0.59 vs. 0.55), suggesting that learning the full composition vector produces useful feature representations beyond the dominant class.

These results establish that continuous mineral composition is learnable from stone photographs, and that the regression framing is practically competitive with direct classification. Current accuracy is not yet sufficient for clinical deployment, but the approach provides a foundation for future work with larger datasets and extension to in vivo endoscopic imaging.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}

\subsection*{Kidney stone disease and the importance of composition}

Kidney stone disease is one of the most common urological conditions worldwide, with incidence rates ranging from 1\% in parts of Asia to 13\% in North America and a global trend of rising prevalence over the past three decades \cite{Türk2022, Qian2022, Sorokin2017, Stamatelou2023}. In 2021 alone, an estimated 106 million new cases were recorded globally \cite{Qian2022}. Recurrence rates are high---up to 50\% of patients experience a second episode within ten years---and the associated morbidity, from acute renal colic to long-term kidney damage, places a substantial burden on healthcare systems.

Preventing recurrence requires knowing what the stone is made of. Different mineral compositions reflect different underlying metabolic disorders, and treatment decisions---dietary modification, urine alkalinisation, or pharmacotherapy---depend directly on this information \cite{Daudon2016, Corrales2021}. The reference method for compositional analysis is Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR), which can accurately identify the chemical nature and relative proportions of each mineral component \cite{Basiri2025, Daudon2016}. However, FTIR is performed ex vivo after the stone has been retrieved and requires specialised laboratory equipment and trained personnel. Results typically take days to weeks to reach the clinician, limiting its utility for timely treatment decisions.

\subsection*{Towards automated visual recognition}

An alternative is to estimate composition from the stone's visual appearance. Clinicians with extensive training can infer composition from surface morphology, texture, and colour---an approach known as endoscopic stone recognition (ESR), formalised in the Daudon morpho-constitutional classification system \cite{Daudon2016, Corrales2021}. In practice, however, ESR requires years of experience and shows poor agreement between observers \cite{Randall2023}, which makes it unsuitable for routine use.

This is where deep learning becomes relevant. Over the past five years, several groups have shown that convolutional neural networks (CNNs) can classify kidney stone types from photographs with reasonable accuracy. Most of this work has focused on in vivo endoscopic images: the group of Ochoa-Ruiz and Daul explored CNN-based classification, two-step transfer learning, and metric learning on ureteroscopic images \cite{LopezTiro2021, LopezTiro2024, GonzalezZapata2024}, while Estrade and colleagues demonstrated recognition of pure and mixed stone morphologies from both still images and video \cite{Estrade2022, Estrade2022video}. Other groups have worked with ex vivo photographs taken after stone extraction: Black et al. were among the first to apply deep learning to this setting \cite{Black2020}, Kim et al. classified ex vivo stone photographs by dominant composition type \cite{Kim2022}, and Serrat et al. built an automated classification pipeline for expelled stones \cite{Serrat2017}. More recently, Zhu et al. trained models on intraoperative images for real-time composition prediction \cite{Zhu2024}, Leng et al. combined segmentation and classification from endoscopic video \cite{Leng2024}, and Onal and Tekgul explored smartphone-based microscopy \cite{Onal2022}. A comprehensive benchmark of these approaches was provided by El Beze et al. \cite{ElBeze2022}.

\subsection*{The gap: classification vs. continuous composition}

Despite this progress, all existing approaches frame the problem as a \textit{classification} task. The model assigns each stone to a fixed category---a single mineral type or a predefined mixture type from the Daudon system---and outputs a discrete label. This formulation discards an important part of the clinical picture: the continuous mineralogical composition measured by FTIR.

Most kidney stones are in fact mixtures of two or more minerals, and the relative proportions matter clinically \cite{Daudon2016}. A stone that is 90\% calcium oxalate monohydrate (COM) with 10\% calcium phosphate (CaP) suggests a different metabolic profile than one that is 60\% COM and 40\% CaP, yet both would receive the same label under existing classification systems. To our knowledge, no prior study has attempted to predict continuous FTIR-calibrated mineral composition percentages directly from stone photographs. This gap motivates the present work.

\subsection*{Research question}

The central question of this thesis is: can a deep learning model predict the continuous mineral composition of a kidney stone from its ex vivo photograph, as calibrated by FTIR spectroscopy?

We address this through two sub-questions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item How accurately can a binary classifier distinguish pure from mixed stones based on visual features alone?
    \item How well does a compositional regression model predict the dominant mineral class compared to a conventional multiclass classifier trained on the same data?
\end{enumerate}

We work with ex vivo photographs---taken immediately after stone retrieval, outside the body---as a deliberate first step before tackling the harder in vivo endoscopic setting. Prior work from this group demonstrated the feasibility of deep learning classification of kidney stones from ex vivo photographs \cite{veltheer2025}. The present study is the first to apply deep learning to the MOCKI dataset and extends the task from discrete classification to continuous compositional prediction.

\section*{Methods}
% ============================================================

\subsection*{Study design}

We trained and compared three deep learning models, each addressing a different aspect of the composition profiling problem (Figure~\ref{fig:workflow}):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Model A}: a binary classifier that distinguishes pure from mixed stones,
    \item \textbf{Model B}: a compositional regression model that predicts continuous mineral percentages,
    \item \textbf{Model C}: a multiclass classifier that predicts the dominant mineral class.
\end{itemize}

Model A addresses the most basic clinical question: does this stone contain a mixture of minerals? This is relevant because pure and mixed stones may require different treatment strategies and follow-up. Model B is the central contribution of this thesis. It is an attempt to predict continuous FTIR-calibrated composition from photographs rather than a categorical label. Model C exists as a controlled baseline for Model B: both models can be evaluated on dominant class prediction, isolating the effect of the regression framing from the architecture and training procedure.


All three models share the same backbone architecture and training procedure; they differ only in their output head and loss function. The full pipeline is config-driven and publicly available at \url{https://github.com/Dirkdeboer/umc_thesis}.

\subsection*{Dataset}

The dataset consists of 416 kidney stone fragments collected at Amsterdam UMC (VUmc) as part of the MOCKI study. Each fragment was photographed ex vivo immediately after retrieval, yielding three photographs per stone (1248 images total). Stones were placed on a black background and photographed under controlled lighting with a digital camera. Two stones were excluded due to missing FTIR labels, leaving 414 stones with valid FTIR-calibrated composition vectors.

Mineral composition was determined by FTIR spectroscopy following standard morpho-constitutional analysis \cite{Daudon2016}, producing continuous percentage values for each component present in the stone. The raw FTIR output distinguishes nine mineral classes.

\subsection*{Data preprocessing}

The raw clinical data required four cleaning steps before it could be used for modelling. Fragment identifiers were forward-filled across photo rows, as only the first photo of each stone carried the identifier in the original spreadsheet. Known data-entry errors in the percentage columns were corrected based on clinical verification (six stones). Photo identifiers with formatting inconsistencies---missing prefixes, single-letter abbreviations---were standardised, and trailing whitespace in component labels was stripped.

\subsubsection*{Class consolidation}

We consolidated the nine raw FTIR classes into six target classes through a two-stage remapping. In the first stage, we examined stones labelled as ``OTH'' (other) using the FTIR sub-identification field. Where the mineral could be chemically identified, we reassigned it to the appropriate class: urate salts and uric acid dihydrate (30 stones) were assigned to UA, calcium-magnesium phosphate (4 stones) to CaP, and newberyite (1 stone) to MAP. The remaining OTH stones---calcite, protein, and xanthine---stayed as OTH because they have no clear chemical relationship to the major classes. In the second stage, we merged subtypes into final classes: COM and COD into CaOx, CHP, CHPD, and CMP into CaP, with UA, MAP, and CYS remaining as individual classes.

For Models B and C, the OTH class was excluded because it is chemically heterogeneous and too small (4 stones with OTH as dominant component) to train or evaluate reliably, leaving 5 target classes across 410 stones.

\subsubsection*{Composition vectors and purity labels}

For each stone, we built a composition vector by summing the FTIR percentages from all positions (primary, secondary, tertiary) into the corresponding class bucket, then normalising the vector to sum to 1.0. This normalised vector serves as the regression target for Model B, and its argmax provides the dominant class label for Model C.

Stones whose composition was 100\% a single mineral were labelled \textit{pure}; all others were labelled \textit{mixed}. This strict threshold was chosen in consultation with the urologists at Amsterdam UMC, who consider any detectable mixture clinically relevant. Prior literature has used a more lenient threshold of 90\% \cite{Kim2022}; we evaluate Model A at both thresholds and compare the effect on classification performance.

\subsubsection*{Data splitting}
 
All splits were performed at the stone level to prevent data leakage: the three photographs of the same stone always end up in the same split. For each model we held out 20\% of stones as a test set (stratified sampling), then split the remaining 80\% into five stratified folds for cross-validation. The stratification variable matched each model's target---binary label for Model A, dominant mineral class for Models B and C.

Because Models B and C share the same 410-stone pool, the same stratification variable, and the same random seed, their fold assignments and test sets are identical. This means we can compare them fold-by-fold rather than just on the aggregated test set. Model A uses all 414 stones with a different stratification, so its test set is not directly comparable. The held-out test set was used only for the final evaluation reported in this thesis.

\subsection*{Image preprocessing and augmentation}

Images were resized to $224 \times 224$ pixels. Before normalisation, we applied a background masking step: pixels whose mean RGB intensity fell below a threshold of 20/255 were zeroed out. This prevents the model from learning features of the black imaging plate rather than the stone surface. After masking, pixel values were normalised using ImageNet channel statistics.

During training, we applied data augmentation to increase the effective dataset size: random horizontal and vertical flips (each with probability 0.5), random rotation up to 45 degrees, and colour jitter (brightness and contrast $\pm$0.4, saturation $\pm$0.2). Because stones are photographed ex vivo on a flat surface, their orientation is arbitrary, which justifies the aggressive geometric augmentation. Colour jitter accounts for variation in lighting across imaging sessions. No augmentation was applied during validation or testing.

\subsection*{Model architecture}

All three models use ResNet18 \cite{He2016} pretrained on ImageNet as their backbone. ResNet18 is an 18-layer residual network with skip connections that enable stable training of deep networks. We chose ResNet18 over the larger ResNet50---used in most prior kidney stone studies \cite{LopezTiro2024, veltheer2025}---because with only 414 stones (410 for the composition models), the larger model is prone to overfitting. The backbone's original classification head is removed, leaving a 512-dimensional feature vector from the global average pooling layer. Each model attaches its own task-specific output head to this feature vector.

\subsubsection*{Model A: binary classifier}

Model A adds a dropout layer followed by a single linear layer that maps the feature vector to two outputs (pure vs. mixed), trained with weighted cross-entropy loss. To address the class imbalance at the 100\% purity threshold---where mixed stones form 65\% of the dataset---we applied inverse-frequency class weighting to the cross-entropy loss. The weight for each class was set proportional to the inverse of its frequency in the training fold, upweighting pure stones to compensate for their underrepresentation. At inference time, the predicted probability of the mixed class is averaged across the three photos of a stone, and a threshold of 0.5 is applied.

\subsubsection*{Model B: compositional regression}

Model B is the central model of this thesis. It predicts a continuous five-dimensional composition vector, where each element represents the estimated fraction of a mineral class (CaOx, CaP, UA, MAP, CYS). The architecture has two output heads that share the same backbone.

The \textit{composition head} maps the feature vector through a hidden layer (512 $\to$ 128 units with ReLU and dropout) to five outputs, which are then passed through a softmax function:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{c}_k = \frac{\exp(z_k)}{\sum_{j=1}^{5} \exp(z_j)}, \quad k = 1, \ldots, 5
\end{equation}
This enforces the compositional constraint: all predicted fractions are non-negative and sum to 1, making them directly comparable to the FTIR composition vectors. It is important to note that this softmax serves as a simplex constraint, not as a classification mechanism---the task is regression on a compositional target, not classification.

The composition head is trained with Kullback--Leibler (KL) divergence between the predicted and true composition:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{KL}} = \sum_{k=1}^{5} c_k \log \frac{c_k}{\hat{c}_k}
\end{equation}
Composition targets with a true fraction of zero are handled by the conventional $0 \log 0 = 0$ definition; the softmax output of the composition head is bounded away from zero by construction, so the loss is numerically stable. We chose KL divergence over simpler alternatives like mean absolute error because it is the natural loss for probability distributions on the simplex. In practical terms, KL divergence penalises confidently wrong predictions more heavily than small errors in already-uncertain classes, which is desirable when the model needs to get the dominant component right while still capturing minority fractions. Each per-class KL term is further scaled by an inverse-frequency weight computed from the training pool, upweighting the contribution of rare classes (MAP, CYS, UA) relative to the dominant CaOx class.

The \textit{auxiliary presence head} has the same architecture but outputs independent sigmoid activations, one per class, predicting whether each mineral is present in the stone. This head is trained with binary cross-entropy loss and provides an additional training signal that encourages the backbone to learn features sensitive to minority components, that might otherwise be ignored because their fractions are small. The total loss combines both terms with a weight of 0.3 on the auxiliary loss. This value was selected from \{0.0, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0\} based on validation MAE and macro F1
(Appendix~\ref{app:lambda}).

At inference time, composition predictions from the three photos of a stone are averaged and re-normalised to sum to 1.

\subsubsection*{Model C: multiclass classifier}

Model C has the same architecture as Model A (dropout + linear) but outputs five logits, one per mineral class. The target label is the dominant component in the FTIR composition vector. Because the dataset is imbalanced---CaOx accounts for 49\% of stones while MAP represents less than 3\%---we used inverse-frequency class weighting in the cross-entropy loss to prevent the model from simply predicting the majority class.

Model C serves as a direct baseline for Model B. Both models can be evaluated on dominant class prediction, which isolates the question of whether predicting continuous compositions offers any advantage over predicting a single class label directly.

At inference time, class probabilities from the three photos are averaged and the class with the highest mean probability is selected.

\subsection*{Training procedure}

All models were trained using a two-phase staged fine-tuning strategy, following the approach used in prior kidney stone classification work \cite{LopezTiro2024, veltheer2025}. In the first phase, we froze the backbone and trained only the output head for 5 epochs. This gives the head a chance to learn a reasonable mapping from ImageNet features before the backbone weights are modified. In the second phase, we unfroze the entire network and trained end-to-end with a much lower learning rate for the backbone than for the head. The idea is that the pre-trained features are already useful and should be adjusted gently rather than overwritten. Training continued for up to 20 epochs (Model A) or 25 epochs (Models B and C) with early stopping based on validation loss. A summary of all hyperparameters is given in Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams}.

\subsection*{Evaluation}

All metrics are computed at the stone level after aggregating predictions across the three photos.

For Model A, we report AUC-ROC, precision, recall, and F1 score. For Model B, we report the mean absolute error (MAE) between predicted and true composition vectors, both per class and overall. To compare Model B directly against Model C, we also derive a dominant class prediction from Model B by taking the class with the highest predicted fraction, and compute macro-F1 and per-class F1 for both models on this shared task. For Model C, we report macro-F1 and per-class F1.

For each model, we also report a random-label baseline generated by training an identical model on data where stone labels had been randomly permuted at the stone level---all three photographs of a stone receive the same shuffled label, preserving within-stone consistency while destroying any relationship between visual features and composition. The baseline model is then trained and evaluated using the same procedure as the main model. Comparison against this baseline confirms that trained performance reflects genuine visual feature learning rather than distributional artefacts or data leakage.

All code, configuration files, and training scripts are publicly available at \url{https://github.com/Dirkdeboer/umc_thesis}. The image data and FTIR labels are held at Amsterdam UMC and are not publicly available, but may be accessible on request for academic research.

\section*{Results}
 
\subsection*{Dataset characteristics}
 
After preprocessing and class consolidation, the dataset comprised 414 stones with valid composition vectors across six mineral classes including OTH (Figure~\ref{fig:presence}). The four OTH-dominant stones are excluded for the composition models (Models B and C), leaving five target classes across 410 stones. CaOx was the dominant mineral in 199 stones (48\%) and CaP in 121 (29\%), together accounting for 77\% of dominant-class assignments. UA, MAP, CYS, and OTH each appeared as the primary component in fewer than 10\% of stones. Notably, CaP also appeared as a secondary or tertiary component in 109 stones (26\%)---compared to only 39 stones (9\%) for CaOx---highlighting how frequently CaP co-occurs in mixed compositions.
 
At the 100\% purity threshold, 146 stones (35\%) were labelled pure and 268 (65\%) mixed. At the 90\% threshold, this shifted to 229 pure (55\%) and 185 mixed (45\%), illustrating how sensitive the class balance is to the threshold definition.

\subsection*{Model A: binary classification of pure vs. mixed stones}

Model A achieved an AUC of 0.750 $\pm$ 0.026 on cross-validation at the 100\% purity threshold, compared to 0.719 $\pm$ 0.110 at the 90\% threshold (Figure~\ref{fig:roc}). Both thresholds substantially outperformed the random-label baseline (AUC = 0.490 $\pm$ 0.080), confirming that the model captures genuine visual features related to stone purity. On the held-out test set, AUC = 0.715 at the 100\% threshold and 0.723 at 90\%. The marginal test-set reversal---where the 90\% threshold edges ahead---is consistent with the much wider fold-to-fold variance at that threshold (std = 0.110 vs.\ 0.026), which makes single held-out estimates noisier.

The 100\% threshold performed slightly better and with lower variance across folds. This may seem counterintuitive---a stricter threshold means fewer pure stones, making the task harder---but it also makes the distinction sharper: a stone is pure only if it contains absolutely no secondary minerals, giving the model a cleaner signal to learn from. At 90\%, the boundary between pure and mixed becomes fuzzier, and stones near the threshold may look visually similar regardless of which side they fall on.

Even with inverse-frequency class weighting, the model shows residual bias toward the majority class at each threshold (Figure~\ref{fig:confusion}). At the 100\% threshold, recall on mixed stones remains higher than on pure stones (79\% vs. 44\%); at the 90\% threshold, the pattern reverses (77\% pure recall vs. 58\% mixed). This residual asymmetry reflects the visual difficulty of the task near the boundary: stones that are borderline pure or mixed look nearly identical regardless of which side of the threshold they fall on. Class weighting can shift the decision boundary but cannot recover visual information that is not present in the image.

\subsection*{Model B: compositional regression}
 
The composition model learned to predict continuous mineral fractions with varying accuracy across classes (Figure~\ref{fig:violin}). On the held-out test set, CaOx showed the strongest performance (MAE = 0.295, $\rho$ = 0.67), followed by CaP (MAE = 0.260, $\rho$ = 0.51). This is consistent with these two classes together comprising 78% of dominant-class assignments (CaOx: 49%, CaP: 29%) and having the widest range of true fractions for the model to learn from. UA, MAP, and CYS had lower MAE values (0.169, 0.078, and 0.107 respectively), partly because their true fractions are zero in stones where these minerals are absent, but their rank correlations were moderate to strong ($\rho$ = 0.45--0.60), suggesting the model captures the relative ordering of stones even for minority classes.
 
The violin plot reveals an important asymmetry in the errors. CaOx predictions are skewed below zero, meaning the model tends to underestimate calcium oxalate fractions. CaP shows the opposite pattern---a positive bias, with the model overestimating calcium phosphate. Since CaOx and CaP frequently co-occur in mixed stones and their fractions must sum to 1 (along with the other classes), this suggests the model struggles to correctly partition the composition between these two dominant classes. For the minority classes (UA, MAP, CYS), errors are more symmetric and concentrated near zero.

For CaOx, the two models perform comparably with Model C marginally ahead (F1 = 0.68 vs. 0.69), which is expected given that calcium oxalate is the largest class and visually distinctive. \section*{Discussion}
 
We set out to investigate whether continuous mineral composition can be predicted from ex vivo kidney stone photographs, as an alternative to the discrete classification framing used in all prior automated approaches. The answer is yes, with important caveats. Model B learns to rank mineral fractions with Spearman correlations of up to 0.67 for calcium oxalate and 0.51 for calcium phosphate, and achieves dominant-class macro F1 on par with a dedicated multiclass classifier (0.483 vs. 0.430 on the held-out test set). These results establish compositional regression as a viable and informative task, even on a dataset of the size available here.
 
\subsection*{Binary classification depends on the purity threshold}
 
Model A achieved AUC values of 0.715 and 0.723 on the held-out test set at the 100\% and 90\% purity thresholds, respectively, with cross-validation AUCs of 0.750 $\pm$ 0.026 and 0.719 $\pm$ 0.110 (Figure~\ref{fig:roc}). On the surface these numbers look similar, but the confusion matrices (Figure~\ref{fig:confusion}) tell a more informative story. At the 100\% threshold, where mixed stones represent 65\% of test stones, the model correctly identifies 79\% of mixed stones while correctly classifying only 16 of 36 pure stones (44\% recall). When the threshold is relaxed to 90\%, the class balance inverts to 55\% pure and the bias flips: 77\% recall on pure, 58\% on mixed.
 
This pattern is a systematic consequence of moderate class imbalance. The model is leaning toward whichever class dominates, rather than learning a truly threshold-invariant visual feature for purity. The substantially higher fold-to-fold variance at the 90\% threshold (AUC std = 0.110 vs. 0.026) is also telling: near-boundary stones---those with 89--91\% of a single mineral---look virtually identical regardless of which side of the cutoff they fall on, introducing label noise that destabilises training.
 
From a clinical standpoint, the 100\% threshold is the more defensible choice. The urologists at Amsterdam UMC consider any detectable mixture clinically relevant to the lithogenic process \cite{Daudon2014}, and the tighter definition produces a cleaner training signal. At neither threshold does Model A reach the precision--recall balance needed for reliable standalone deployment, but its AUC of 0.715 demonstrates that genuine visual information about stone purity exists in ex vivo photographs and is recoverable from moderate-scale training data.
 
\subsection*{Compositional regression captures real structure}
 
The central question of this thesis was whether a model can predict continuous FTIR composition from a photograph. Model B's results provide an encouraging answer. A Spearman correlation of 0.67 for CaOx means the model correctly orders stones along the calcium oxalate fraction axis in two-thirds of pairwise comparisons. For CaP ($\rho$ = 0.51) and the three minority classes ($\rho$ = 0.45--0.60), the ordering signal is weaker but clearly above chance. As a reference: a model that always predicts the training mean composition achieves $\rho$ = 0 by construction, and one predicting the per-class mean achieves slightly positive $\rho$ but no within-class discrimination. Model B's correlations go beyond what class membership alone can explain.
 
The absolute errors are larger than would be needed for clinical use. An MAE of 0.295 for CaOx corresponds to predictions being off by 30 percentage points on average, which cannot reliably distinguish a 60\% CaOx stone from a 90\% stone. This is the honest current limitation of the approach. However, it should be read alongside the dataset constraints: 414 stones is small by deep learning standards, and much of the training signal is concentrated in the two dominant classes. The error level is consistent with what Ruf et al.\ observed in the methodologically analogous task of predicting mineral fractions from geological drill core images at comparable dataset sizes \cite{Ruf2024}; their study also showed that per-mineral MAE decreases substantially as training data grows.
 
The per-class error distributions reveal an important structural pattern (Figure~\ref{fig:violin}). CaOx predictions are biased downward while CaP predictions are biased upward. These two classes co-occur in over half the stones in our dataset, and the softmax simplex constraint means that overestimating one necessarily depresses the other. What the model appears to learn is a reasonable dominant-class signal for CaOx and CaP separately, but it has insufficient training examples of intermediate mixed compositions---say, 40\% CaOx and 40\% CaP---to partition the two fractions accurately when both are large. A similar inter-mineral confusion between co-occurring classes was reported by Ruf et al.\ \cite{Ruf2024}, and by the Ochoa-Ruiz group for in vivo images where COM and CHP co-occur \cite{LopezTiro2024}. In clinical terms, this CaOx--CaP confusion has real consequences: Daudon has argued that even minor CaP content can indicate specific metabolic dysfunctions such as tubular acidification defects, where the difference between 10\% and 40\% secondary CaP carries distinct diagnostic weight \cite{Daudon2014}.
 
For the minority classes, the model achieves lower MAE values (UA = 0.169, MAP = 0.078, CYS = 0.107) partly because stones in which these minerals are absent have a true fraction of zero by definition, leaving less room for large absolute errors. The moderate correlations suggest the model captures enough visual signal to rank stones within these classes, though the test set contains only 2 MAP-dominant stones and dominant-class F1 for that class cannot be interpreted.
 
\subsection*{Regression matches classification, and sometimes beats it}
 
Comparing Model B and Model C on the shared task of dominant class prediction directly addresses the second sub-question. Both achieved macro F1 above 0.40 on the test set (0.483 and 0.430 for Models B and C, respectively), 4.0 and 3.6 times the random-label baseline (0.120). The question is whether the regression framing actually buys anything beyond direct classification.
 
For CaOx, the two models perform comparably, with Model C marginally ahead (F1 = 0.68 vs. 0.69). For CaP, Model B outperforms Model C (F1 = 0.44 vs. 0.38). This is a substantive result: by optimising the full composition vector rather than a categorical label, Model B develops a richer internal representation of CaP stones that transfers to the dominant-class task. Learning \textit{how much} CaP is present---even when CaOx is the dominant class---helps the model correctly identify stones where CaP takes over as the majority component. Model C, which only ever sees the dominant label as a target, has no mechanism to learn this.
 
Model B also edges out Model C on CYS (F1 = 0.59 vs. 0.55). We do not draw a conclusion from MAP, where the test set holds too few dominant stones for the F1 to be meaningful. The CYS result is suggestive rather than definitive given the small number of cystine stones, but it points to a plausible mechanism: by learning how much of each mineral is present, the composition objective may force the model to represent even rare components in its feature space, which would carry over to dominant-class prediction.
 
The net picture is that Model B offers everything Model C offers for majority classes, plus a continuous composition output that carries additional information beyond the dominant label---all at the same classification cost. This confirms the practical motivation for the regression framing.
 
\subsection*{Clinical relevance and context}
 
The clinical motivation for this work is that stone composition is not a categorical variable, yet the existing approaches treat it as one. Daudon showed that FTIR-based composition percentages contain clinically relevant information beside the dominant class: the ratio of COM to COD within the calcium oxalate fraction indicates oxaluria severity, and the proportion of CaP sub-types reflects tubular acidification status \cite{Daudon2014}.
 
The current accuracy is not sufficient for clinical deployment. However, the approach has value as a screening tool: a model predicting that a stone is 70\% CaOx with 25\% CaP secondary provides more information than a label of ``calcium oxalate stone,'' even if the fractions are imprecise. Real-time approximate composition estimates during ureteroscopy---before the stone is retrieved for FTIR---could guide intraoperative decisions on fragmentation technique and sampling. Model B matches a dedicated classifier on dominant-class prediction while also providing continuous composition estimates. This confirms that the regression framing is practically viable---even at this dataset size and without any architectural complexity beyond a standard ResNet18.
 
\subsection*{Comparison to prior work}
 
No prior study has attempted continuous composition prediction from kidney stone photographs, so direct quantitative comparison with the literature is not possible. Our Model C classification results provide a conventional benchmark on the same data.
 
Kim et al.\ applied multiclass deep learning to ex vivo stone photographs, classifying 965 stones into four composition groups (pure COM, COM+struvite, COM+COD, and pure UA), and reported per-class sensitivity of 85--95\% \cite{Kim2022}. Black et al.\ trained ResNet-101 on 63 ex vivo stone images across five mineral classes (COM, UA, struvite, brushite, cystine) and reported a weighted recall of 85\% \cite{Black2020}. The Ochoa-Ruiz group performed multiclass identification of six stone types from in vivo endoscopic images using two-step transfer learning (pretraining on ex vivo images, fine-tuning on endoscopic images) and reported weighted accuracy up to 97\% \cite{LopezTiro2021, LopezTiro2024}. Veltheer applied a similar two-step approach on Serrat's ex vivo myStone dataset (six Daudon morphological classes) with a ResNet50 backbone \cite{veltheer2025}. Direct numerical comparison with any of these results is not meaningful: the studies differ in number of classes (4--6 vs.\ our 5), dataset, imaging modality, and evaluation metric, and none reports macro F1 on a comparable five-class ex vivo task.
 
The closest methodological parallel for our regression approach is Ruf et al., who predicted continuous mineral fractions from geological drill core images using a pretrained CNN, on a dataset of 361 labeled image segments \cite{Ruf2024}. Their findings---that co-occurring minerals with similar visual textures are hardest to separate, and that overall MAE is consistent with our range---independently validate the feasibility of the compositional regression formulation in image-based mineral analysis, despite the domain difference.
 
\subsection*{Limitations}
 
Four limitations should be noted. The dataset remains small by the standards of deep learning: 414 stones limits what can be learned about minority classes and intermediate mixed compositions. MAP has only 12 dominant-class stones in the full dataset, and even CYS has 39; the test sets for these classes are too small for stable metric estimates.
 
All images were acquired ex vivo under controlled conditions at a single institution. In vivo endoscopic images present substantially different challenges---partial stone visibility, variable lighting, blood and tissue in the field---and our results do not directly generalise to that setting. Single-institution data also limits the generalisability of the composition distribution; Amsterdam UMC's patient population may not reflect the prevalence of different stone types in other healthcare settings.
 
We did not perform extensive hyperparameter optimisation beyond the auxiliary loss weight sweep. In particular, we did not ablate the backbone architecture, the augmentation strategy, or the choice of KL divergence versus MAE as the primary regression loss. Given the tight deadline constraints, these were left as open questions for future work. Finally, we have no outcome data linking our composition predictions to clinical decisions or patient outcomes, which would ultimately be needed to demonstrate translational utility.
 
\subsection*{Future directions}
 
The most impactful extension of this work would be to in vivo endoscopic images, where real-time composition estimation could directly inform treatment during ureteroscopy. The two-step transfer learning strategy developed in this group \cite{veltheer2025}---pretraining on ex vivo images before fine-tuning on in vivo data---provides a natural path for that extension, and our composition model could serve as the ex vivo pretrain stage.
 
On the modelling side, two improvements are within reach without additional data. First, the KL divergence loss already applies inverse-frequency class weighting; future work could explore more aggressive weighting schemes or a focal-loss variant that further down-weights easy majority-class predictions, which may yield additional gains for rare classes. Second, a hybrid loss that combines weighted KL for composition regression with an auxiliary dominant-class head could reinforce the rare-class signal while preserving the full-composition output.
 
Beyond model improvements, the highest-impact investment would be a larger and more compositionally diverse dataset. The CaOx--CaP confusion we observe is a structural consequence of the model seeing too few examples of intermediate mixed compositions; more stones with 30--60\% fractions of both classes would directly address this. Multi-institution data collection, potentially coordinated through the networks established by the Ochoa-Ruiz and Daudon groups, would also enable the external validation that single-institution studies cannot provide.

\section*{Conclusion}
 
This thesis asked whether a deep learning model can predict the continuous mineral composition of a kidney stone from its ex vivo photograph. The answer is yes, in a meaningful but bounded sense. Model B learns to rank mineral fractions with Spearman correlations of 0.67 for calcium oxalate and 0.51 for calcium phosphate, and achieves dominant-class macro F1 on par with a dedicated multiclass classifier despite never being trained on categorical labels. This demonstrates that FTIR-calibrated compositional information is recoverable from photographic appearance, and that framing the problem as regression rather than classification is not only theoretically sound but practically viable.
 
Addressing the first sub-question, Model A distinguishes pure from mixed stones with AUC values of 0.715 (100\% threshold) and 0.723 (90\% threshold), confirming that a genuine purity signal exists in the visual features of ex vivo photographs. Performance is constrained by class imbalance, with the model biased toward the majority class at both thresholds. The 100\% purity definition produces more stable cross-validation results and is more clinically defensible, as any detectable mixture is considered relevant for treatment decisions.
 
Addressing the second sub-question, the compositional regression model matches the multiclass classifier on overall dominant-class macro F1 (0.483 vs. 0.430) and outperforms it on calcium phosphate (F1 = 0.44 vs. 0.38) and uric acid (F1 = 0.46 vs. 0.35). It shows a smaller advantage on cystine (F1 = 0.59 vs. 0.55), while the MAP test set is too small to support a comparison. Together, these results show that the regression framing is at least as strong as direct classification for dominant-class prediction, and better on calcium phosphate and uric acid, while also providing the continuous composition output that motivates the approach.
 
The primary bottleneck is dataset size. With 414 stones (410 for the composition models), rare-class learning is limited and the model sees too few intermediate mixed compositions to accurately partition co-occurring minerals. Extending this work to larger, multi-institutional datasets and ultimately to in vivo endoscopic images are the key next steps toward clinical translation.
